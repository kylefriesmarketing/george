# G FOR GEORGE — The Keeper's Bible

*A branching playable book of the true Great Escape — Stalag Luft III, 1943–45 — for THE SHELF.
The events are true; the names are changed. Written to be handed to a fresh session so it can
build, not re-decide. Trust this file; verify only where the code visibly disagrees.*

Working title **G FOR GEORGE** — the hero's Lancaster's call sign, and the name of the real
fourth tunnel dug under the camp theater after the Fifty (historical fact: it was called George).
Our fiction supplies only the *reason* for the name. One phrase that holds the whole game: the
aircraft that fell, the tunnel of defiance, the friend you're trying to get home. Strong
alternates, same game: **THE SPORT** (from the real notice posted in the camps afterward:
*"escape from prison camps is no longer a sport"*) or **WIRE**. Repo/folder: `george`. Live
target: `https://kylefriesmarketing.github.io/george/` · a book on THE ROOM hub.

Sits on the shelf as the **true-war document**, built the SOUTH way: a real ordeal, played from
inside, where the big history is fixed and the branching is *your thread through it*. SOUTH is
leadership against nature; STILL BREATHING is the body against cold; G FOR GEORGE is **hope
against arithmetic** — the first shelf game where the core meter *builds* instead of drains, and
the first where you play toward an ending the narrator already knows.

---

## 1. The pitch, and why it is UNIQUE on this shelf

October 1942. Your Lancaster, G-George, goes down over Essen. Five of your crew die in her. You
and your navigator, Freddie, float down into Germany — and by spring 1943 you are in the North
Compound of **Stalag Luft III**, Sagan, Silesia: the Luftwaffe's escape-proof camp, built on
yellow sand that betrays every tunnel. Six hundred prisoners are about to answer that with the
most audacious escape organization of the war: three tunnels at once, named Tom, Dick, and Harry.

Every other SHELF game runs on meters that drain — breath, hope, sanity, blood. G FOR GEORGE is
built around the shelf's first **constructive meter**:

- **THE TUNNEL.** A persistent bar measured in feet — Harry needs **336 of them**, thirty feet
  down, from the stove in Hut 104 to the pine treeline. Every choice can feed it (bed boards,
  sand bags, forged stamps, a tamed guard) or endanger it. Watching that number grow across a
  year of camp-time is the game: **hope made physical, an inch at a time.** No shelf game has
  ever let the player *build* something.
- **A true war story with almost no combat.** The whole game happens inside 200 yards of wire.
  The antagonists are boredom, sand, winter, a microphone buried by the fence, and a professional
  ferret who is very good at his job. Tension without a single battle scene.
- **The cruelest mechanic is a list of names.** The Roster: two hundred places in the escape
  order, drawn by lottery — as really happened — and order decides survival odds. There is one
  early place between you and Freddie. That decision is the game's beating heart.
- **You play toward a night that really happened.** March 24–25, 1944: the frozen trap, the
  tunnel surfacing ten feet short of the trees, the air-raid blackout, the rope signal, the shot
  at man 77. Seventy-six men out. Seventy-three recaptured. **Fifty murdered.** Three home. The
  history is fixed; *where your number falls in it is not* — and the narrator, telling it fifty
  years later, has spent his whole life dealing himself different numbers.

The genre no SHELF title occupies: **the patience thriller / the true-war elegy.** The boys'-own
adventure that history turns into a war crime, told by the last man who remembers the jokes.

## 2. History & honor — the trust section (nail this so the build session never panics)

**The events are true. The names are changed. Keep the contract bright and state it to the
player** (title page: *"This happened. The names belong to no one, so that the story can belong
to all of them."* — and the afterword gives the real record).

- **The camp, the compound, the tunnels, the night are real.** Stalag Luft III at Sagan; North
  Compound, opened spring 1943; Tom, Dick, and Harry begun almost at once; Tom found and blown up
  September '43; Dick demoted to a sand dump and kit store when the forest west of the wire was
  cleared for camp expansion; Harry pushed through the winter and finished in March '44. The
  escape night events in §8 are the documented record, beat for beat. The fourth tunnel, George,
  dug under the theater after the escape, is real too — that's ours to give a reason.
- **Every named character is a renamed or composite counterpart** of the real men, and their
  fates track the record (§7 maps each). The protagonist and his navigator are composites — the
  camp held hundreds of such crews — so their personal thread can branch freely without
  rewriting any real man's life. **Iron rule: where a character tracks a real man, never invent
  dishonor for him, and never let player agency change his recorded fate.** The branching lives
  in the composite thread (Kit, Freddie, the player's choices), never in the history.
- **The Fifty are depicted as the camp experienced them**: rumor, then a posted list that grew
  for a week, then urns coming back by rail. Offscreen, factual, never aestheticized. The real
  prisoners built a memorial vault down the Sagan road — it stands today — and the game ends
  with the afterword page **"The Fifty"**: the true account, the real names our characters wore
  (Bushell, Walenn, and the rest), the three who made it home, and the memorial. Closing line:
  *"The names in this story were changed. These were the real ones."* That page is the game's
  spine and its license to exist.
- **What to avoid** (separate, live copyrights — do not reference or reskin): the 1963 film *The
  Great Escape* (no motorcycle jump, no baseball-in-the-cooler — film inventions, not history),
  Paul Brickhill's book's specific prose and scene construction (the events are public record;
  his sentences are not), *The Wooden Horse*, TV's *Colditz* and *Hogan's Heroes* (no
  comedy-Nazi register, ever). **100% original prose**, built on the documented record.
- **Dignity rules.** Germans are humans, not cartoons: a decent old-school Kommandant who paid
  for the escape with his command and a court-martial (true), a professional ferret who attended
  prisoner reunions after the war (true), a homesick corporal — and, offstage, the Gestapo,
  which is where the game keeps its true darkness. No swastika glamor; iconography stays minimal
  and period-drab. Violence is aftermath and paperwork: the worst things in this game are a
  notice, a list, and twelve small boxes on a railway platform.

## 3. The hard problem, and the core loop

**The problem:** The ending is famous, the daily texture is repetitive, and the player's agency
must live inside fixed history without feeling like a rail. Make it a stealth-management sim and
you lose the book feel; make it pure narrative and the year of digging collapses into a montage.

**The solution — three moves:**

1. **The tunnel is the clock, the meter, and the character.** Progress in feet is always
   visible, always earnable, always at risk. Chapters are camp-seasons, and each season asks:
   what did you feed the tunnel this month, and what did it cost? Camp life's repetitive texture
   becomes the *point* — ritual, not filler — because every ritual either feeds the tunnel or
   protects it.
2. **Two currencies, one economy: HEAT and NERVE.** Everything that feeds the tunnel raises HEAT
   (ferret suspicion — searches, informers, the buried microphones). Everything that protects
   the men costs tunnel time and feeds NERVE (morale — the theater, the jokes, the brew ritual).
   The player always trades speed against safety against sanity — a triangle small enough to
   feel, not spreadsheet. History fixes *whether* Harry gets finished; the player's economy
   decides **what shape the men are in when it does, and how ready your own thread is** (KIT,
   THE CREW, your lottery standing).
3. **Fixed history, free thread — the SOUTH covenant.** The big beats happen on schedule (Tom
   blown, the winter halt, the escape night, the list). The player's choices decide everything
   history didn't record: your role in the organization, who you carried and who you failed,
   your number in the order, what happens to you and Freddie at the hole and on the roads. And
   because the narrator survived (he's telling it in 1994), the darkest personal endings are
   framed as the counterfactuals he still deals himself at 3 a.m. — *"deal me a different
   number, and that's the whole story"* (§6). Survivor's guilt, made into a game structure.

**The core loop:** MORNING APPELL (the day's weather: searches, news, letters) → **WORK** (choose
where you spend yourself: the tunnel, the kit, the men, the goons) → **COMPLICATION** (a ferret
in the wrong hut, a fall of sand, a letter from home, an informer rumor) → **EVENING** (the hut
at night: Freddie, the brew, the talk — where the bonds and the doubts live) → next day, next
week, next season. Feet accumulate. HEAT accumulates. Winter comes.

**Iron law of the loop:** *the tunnel never grows without a cost, and the cost is never only
yours.* Every foot is someone's bed board, someone's risk, someone's nerve. The game keeps the
ledger honest.

## 4. The instruments (the HUD)

Where NOBODY has the Name/Wrath/Loom and CURIOUSER has SIZE/SELF, G FOR GEORGE has a war ledger —
one thing being built, two pressures, one readiness, one bond:

- **THE TUNNEL** — the star. A horizontal cross-section filling left-to-right, drawn like pencil
  on squared paper: the shaft, the halfway houses (Piccadilly and Leicester Square — their real
  names), the treeline at 336. It tracks the active tunnel: Tom until September (and the player
  feels the bar DIE — months of feet, dynamited), then Harry through the winter. In Act IV,
  George: a bar with **no end mark** — feet dug toward nothing on the map and everything that
  matters. **The player should feel about this bar the way Choose Wisely players feel about the
  Bracelet.**
- **HEAT** — ferret suspicion, 0–10, a thermometer of footprints. Rises with every risk; falls
  with quiet weeks and sacrificial decoys (letting them find what you let them find). At
  thresholds: hut searches, appell shakedowns, the microphones get checked. HEAT is *managed*,
  never zeroed — and history says the ferrets DO find Tom, so the player's HEAT decides what
  *else* they find with it.
- **NERVE** — the men's weather, yours included. Not one number but a small board of states for
  the named cast: *steady → quiet → fevered*. Fed by the theater, the jokes, letters, the brew
  ritual; drained by winter, bad news, reprisals, and the wire. A man who goes *fevered* and is
  left alone drifts toward the wire — and the guards shoot at the wire. (The shelf's Dread/haunt
  register, worn by the whole cast instead of the player.)
- **KIT** — escape readiness, 0–100%: papers (the Travel Agency's forgeries), converted clothes,
  compass, silk maps, German phrases, cover story. KIT doesn't dig an inch — it decides what
  happens *after* the hole. Runners with poor KIT get caught at the first station check. It also
  weighs your lottery standing: the committee really did rank the order by contribution and
  escape-worthiness before the draw.
- **THE CREW** — the bond with Freddie, the game's Bracelet: two silhouettes and the thread
  between them (pilot and navigator, all that's left of G-George's seven). Mended by honesty and
  the brew ritual; frayed by secrets, the Dear John, and the roster. THE CREW's state decides
  the quality of every ending it touches — and it decides what happens at the hole.

**Persistent, cross-run: THE LOG.** Every named man whose full story you witness (his best scene
and his fate) is entered in the camp log, kept across runs — the Truths pattern. The frame story
(§6) knows about it: on a second run the old man says, *"I've told this before. It comes out
different every time. That's not lying — that's the only way to tell all of it."* Replay is
canonically **retelling**. Fill the Log and the true ending (§9) opens.

## 5. THE EMOTIONAL TONE — the four registers (this section is load-bearing)

The game's voice is an old man telling the truth at last, and the truth includes the jokes. Four
registers, deployed in a deliberate arc. **The register discipline IS the game's quality bar.**

**Register 1 — THE LARK (dominant in Act I, present until the list).** POW camps ran on gallows
comedy and elaborate mischief: goon-baiting, ersatz-coffee jokes, decoy escape attempts staged to
waste the ferrets' afternoon, a theater doing drawing-room comedies on sets built from Red Cross
crates. The game must be genuinely funny here — not quips, but the deep structural comedy of
clever men with infinite time and no dignity left to lose. *The jokes are how you know they're
still alive.* Voice sample:

> The Weasel spent four hours under Hut 11 today and found the tin of jam Duke buried for him on
> Tuesday. He confiscated it with tremendous ceremony. Duke says the jam cost him two cigarettes
> and was worth a fortnight of the Weasel's attention. We are at war, and we are winning it two
> cigarettes at a time.

**Register 2 — THE ACHE (rises through Act II).** Tedium, hunger, and home. Letters that take
three months and say too little; a wife's handwriting changing; the Penguin describing his
allotment's marrows to men who dream in vegetables now. The ache is quiet and it accumulates
like sand. Never melodrama — it's the space between the lines of a letter read four times.
Voice sample:

> Nell writes that the garden gate still sticks and that I am not to worry about anything. It is
> the fourth letter in a row in which I am not to worry about anything. I have begun to wonder
> what, precisely, I am not to worry about.

**Register 3 — THE DREAD (spikes at discoveries; owns the escape night and its aftermath).** The
buried microphones. A ferret's torch under the floor at 2 a.m. Two tons of sand and nowhere left
to put it. On the night: a frozen trap, a tunnel ten feet short, and six hundred feet of rope
telegraphing *wait — wait — go* between the hole and the trees. Dread here is procedural, not
supernatural — the terror of a man in a hole hearing boots on the boards above. And then the
aftermath dread, which is worse because it comes in instalments: rumor, then the list. The bell
for this register: **Tiny stops joking.** When the camp's laugh goes quiet, the player knows the
water is deep. Voice sample:

> The list went up under the eagle by the cookhouse door, typed, no explanation. Forty-one names.
> We stood in the cold and read it top to bottom, and nobody said anything, because every man
> was doing the same arithmetic: forty-one is not seventy-three, and the list had room at the
> bottom. It grew all that week. It stopped at fifty. I have never in my life been so afraid of
> a piece of paper.

**Register 4 — THE ELEGY (the frame; owns every ending).** 1994. An RAF club, a reunion, more
empty chairs than full ones. The last man of the Hut 104 crowd, telling it to his friend's
granddaughter because no one else is left to check his facts. The elegy voice is plain, dry, and
devastating by restraint — it earns tears by refusing to ask for them. Every ending card is
written in this voice. Voice sample:

> People ask, knowing how it ended, whether it was worth the digging. I tell them Harry was
> three hundred and thirty-six feet long, and that for a year, every man in that compound lived
> in it — a foot at a time, under the wire, past the dogs — home. Fifty of us paid for it with
> everything there is. And I have yet to meet the man who was there who will tell you we
> shouldn't have dug. That is not the kind of thing you can explain to anyone who wasn't. I'm
> told it's what made us difficult husbands.

**Tone rules (iron):**
1. **Humor is load-bearing, not relief.** Cut a joke and a man dies less believably later. The
   lark register must be good enough to be missed.
2. **Understatement is the house style.** Kriegies wrote "things are a bit thin" about
   starvation winters. The prose never cries; the player does.
3. **Violence is aftermath and paperwork.** A burst at the wire is heard from inside a hut. The
   Fifty is a list under an eagle, and later, small boxes on a railway platform. Never onscreen.
4. **The Germans get their humanity and keep their menace.** Brandt's daughter in Hamburg. The
   Kommandant's first-war photographs. The Weasel's craftsman's pride. And underneath, always,
   the knowledge that there is a uniform worse than the one on the wire, and that losing the
   Kommandant means meeting it. Decency here is real AND fragile — the period truth, and after
   the escape, the plot point: the decent man is arrested for having been decent.
5. **Hope is a discipline, not a mood.** The game's thesis, said once by the Doc and proven by
   the systems, then proven again by George: *"The tunnel doesn't have to reach the trees. It
   has to reach June."* Digging keeps men from the wire. That is why Act IV's George — a tunnel
   dug by grieving men with no plan to use it — is the title of the game.

## 6. The frame — the reunion (how the book opens, closes, and replays)

- **COLD OPEN, 1994:** white tablecloths, a folded RAF blazer of a man not present, a
  granddaughter (Tiny's — she has his laugh) asking the last of the Hut 104 crowd: *"Grandad
  never would tell it. Will you?"* One page. Then: *"October '42. It starts, like everything in
  my life worth telling, with an engine on fire."* → the crash node.
- **The narrator is the player-character, aged 74.** All prose is his telling — first person,
  past tense, the four registers of §5. This licenses chapter-skips ("that winter took a page of
  the calendar and most of my patience"), foreshadow-flickers, and honest unreliability.
- **THE COUNTERFACTUAL LICENSE (how a survivor's story branches into dark endings):** the
  narrator lived — he's in the chair, telling it. But he has spent fifty years dealing himself
  different numbers. When a run ends with Kit dead or taken — the wire, the hole, the list —
  the frame closes on the old man saying so: *"That's how close it came. Deal me a different
  number, and that's the whole story."* The dark endings are the counterfactuals he still
  rehearses at 3 a.m. — survivor's guilt as a narrative engine. (The granddaughter's reactions
  make clear which tellings are memory and which are the arithmetic he can't stop doing.)
- **GAME OVER = the telling ends.** Each ending closes back in the club: the room, her face,
  what he does with his hands. The ending card is his last line, in the elegy register.
- **REPLAY = RETELLING** (the frame's gift, unique on the shelf): a returning player is the old
  man re-telling on another visit. THE LOG (§4) persists; the granddaughter remembers what he
  told her last time and asks about a man he skipped. Completionism is *diegetic*: she is
  collecting his dead for him, and he is grateful and pretending not to be.

## 7. The cast (renamed and composite counterparts of real men — Log entries all)

*Contract: characters marked **[≈ real]** track a real man's role and recorded fate, which player
agency never alters; characters marked **[composite]** are drawn from the many, and their fates
are the game's branching. The afterword prints the real names.*

**The Crew [composite — the player's free thread]:**
- **F/Lt Christopher "Kit" Harland** ({HERO}, renameable) — 22, pilot of G-George, our narrator
  at both ages. Carries the five dead of his crew like a kit bag he won't put down. His arc:
  from "I put Freddie here" guilt to whatever the player makes of the roster.
- **F/O Freddie Rowe** ({FRIEND}, renameable) — 21, navigator, the other half of THE CREW.
  Sunny, precise, keeps everyone's maps and everyone's spirits. Engaged to **Peggy** — until Act
  II's Dear John, after which the navigator who can find anywhere no longer has a destination.
  The roster's knife: by escape night, Kit has everything to go home to, and Freddie has nothing
  — and only one of your two lottery places is early enough in the order to matter.

**The Committee:**
- **W/Cdr Edmund Vane, "the Magpie"** **[≈ Big X — Roger Bushell]** — pre-war barrister;
  collects men, maps, and tin the way other men collect stamps. Magnetic, relentless, honest
  about the arithmetic: *"Every man out the hole is ten thousand Germans looking for him instead
  of shooting at our brothers."* Third escape attempt; the Gestapo had promised him there would
  not be a fourth. He goes anyway, early in the order, as the real man did. **His name is on the
  list.** The game never settles whether he spent lives or saved them — and the narrator has
  had fifty years to try.
- **F/Lt Stefan Zabek** **[≈ the Tunnel King — Wally Floody, with a Polish airman's stakes]** —
  ex-miner, buried alive at sixteen for nine hours and claustrophobic ever since. Digs anyway,
  every day, at the face. Knows better than anyone what recapture means for a Pole. **Fate, as
  the record has it for the real Tunnel King: purged to another compound weeks before the
  escape** — the man who built Harry never went down it, and it saved his life, and he never
  forgave either fact. His Log entry is the game's bravest.
- **F/O Owen "Inky" Price** **[≈ the Forger — Tim Walenn]** — Welsh, gentle, runs "Dean &
  Dawson," the Travel Agency (its real nickname) — reproducing German typefaces by hand, one
  letter at a time, by candlelight, going quietly half-blind for other men's papers. Jokes that
  he's the only man in Germany ruining his eyes to read LESS. Letters to his mam are the ache
  register's home key. **His name is on the list.** The player will know Inky for two acts
  before the week the list grows. This is the game's deepest wound, on purpose.
- **F/O Roy "Duke" Callahan** **[composite scrounger, Canadian]** — charm in a converted
  greatcoat; owns the tame-goon pipeline: chocolate, cigarettes, sympathy — then the hook. His
  blackmail of Brandt is the player's first genuinely dirty choice, and his self-disgust about
  it is his story. Fate branches with the player's night.
- **F/O "Tiny" Watts** **[composite penguin]** — enormous, gentle, funny; disperses sand from
  his trouser bags with a music-hall shuffle; calls the tunnels "the garden — same work, upside
  down." **The camp's tone-bell: while Tiny jokes, the game is a lark.** Survives; his
  granddaughter holds the frame.
- **S/L "Doc" Meredith** **[composite MO]** — the committee's conscience. Tends wire fever;
  carries the thesis line. After the list: it's Doc who proposes George. *"Not to the trees this
  time. Just deep enough to stand up straight in."*

**The Germans:**
- **Oberst von Reyter** **[≈ the Kommandant — von Lindeiner]** — old Luftwaffe, flew in the
  first war, decent within a system that is not; warned the senior officers, almost pleading,
  that escape had stopped being a game (true). After the break: relieved of command and
  court-martialed for having run his camp like a soldier instead of a jailer (true). The game
  shows what his removal cost the prisoners — the roof they didn't know they had.
- **Uffz. Brandt** **[composite tame goon]** — homesick, kind, a daughter in Hamburg. July '43,
  Hamburg burns; the camp watches him read a telegram. What Duke's hook does to this man
  afterward is the cost ledger, itemized.
- **Fw. Griess, "the Weasel"** **[≈ the chief ferret — Glemnitz]** — a craftsman who respects
  craft; the closest thing the game has to a rival player. Finds Tom and is *complimentary
  about the shoring* (the real ferret was). And the frame holds the true, impossible coda: **the
  Weasel came to the reunions after the war, and the prisoners were glad to see him.** His last
  scene with Kit is written with the respect of two professionals who wasted a war on each
  other.

**Home (letters only, and the more present for it):** Kit's mother and sister; **Nell**, Kit's
girl, whose letters are the ACHE register's metronome — and whose final letter, in several
endings, is the payoff the whole game has been addressing.

## 8. The shape of the book (acts, spine nodes, ~65 nodes — fixed history in bold)

**ACT I — THE BAG (Oct '42 – spring '43, ~12 nodes).** The fall: G-George on fire, the jump,
five chutes that don't open in the telling twice the same way. Capture — *"for you the war is
over"* — and the **Dulag Luft interrogation** (real), a name-rank-number gauntlet that teaches
NERVE (the interrogator is friendly; that's the trap). Then Sagan, and in spring, the move into
the **new North Compound** — where the Magpie's organization is planning, from day one, the
biggest thing ever attempted: **three tunnels at once** (real strategy: dig three, so losing one
proves the compound "clean"). The lark register at full volume; the extraordinary society inside
(lectures, a theater going up, a stock exchange in cigarettes). A naive solo scheme fails
educationally — and gets Kit noticed. Act ends: sworn in as a penguin; first sand down the
trouser leg; the tunnel bar appears. *Emotional key: shame → purpose.*

**ACT II — THE TRADE (1943, the long year, ~24 nodes — the game's body).** The build, in
seasons, all of it the documented record: the traps (Harry's under the stove in Hut 104, kept
lit so the concrete stays warm to the touch); the yellow sand problem and the penguins; the
theater rising; **Dick demoted to a sand dump when the Germans clear the forest west of the
wire** — a tunnel that lost its destination and found a purpose (the game underlines it; it's
George's foreshadow). The deep cast scenes live here: Duke's hook in Brandt; the theater's
opening night (cover for the survey team, and the year's NERVE harvest); Zabek's nine hours,
retold at the face; Inky's eyes; **Hamburg burns** (July); Freddie's Dear John — and Peggy's
handwriting was the thing he navigated by. Mid-act gut punch, on history's schedule: **the
ferrets find Tom** (September). Months of feet on the HUD, dynamited before the whole camp —
and the Weasel compliments the shoring. The Magpie, that night: *"Good. They think that was the
best we had."* **Harry halts for winter; the microphones; the January restart; the sand goes
under the theater seats now** (real). A minor named man (Sandy Cole, established two scenes
earlier as wallpaper — deliberately) goes fevered and walks to the wire at dawn, and the game
makes the player hear it from inside the hut. Act ends: **336. Harry is done.** *Emotional key:
endurance; the ledger opens.*

**ACT III — THE ORDER (March 1944, ~14 nodes).** KIT at full pace — the Travel Agency's papers
are DATED (real detail: forged dates forced the schedule); the converted clothes; the cover
stories rehearsed in German. **The lottery at the theater** (real: the first places ranked by
contribution and fluency, the rest drawn — your standing feeds it): your number, and Freddie's,
drawn in front of you. The knife on the table: one of them is early enough to matter, and THE
CREW decides what can even be said about it. The weather argument, the moon argument, and the
Magpie's *"we go."* **THE NIGHT, minute by minute — the record, beat for beat:** the frozen
trap (ninety minutes lost before the first man is even down); **the tunnel breaks TEN FEET SHORT
of the treeline**, in the open, in snow, with a goon tower fifty yards off — and the fix,
improvised at the hole in the dark: a rope from the ladder to a blind behind the trees, one tug
for *wait*, two for *go*; the **air-raid blackout** that kills the tunnel's electric lights with
men crawling in it; the sand falls; the hour the schedule dies (ten a minute planned; four an
hour true). Where YOUR number falls decides your act's ending: out and running, in the tunnel
when the torch swings wrong, or standing at the hut window doing arithmetic as the sky pales.
**4:55 a.m.: the seventy-seventh man is seen. A shot. Seventy-six out.** *Emotional key: the
machine of hope meeting the night as it actually went.*

**ACT IV — THE LIST AND THE GARDEN (spring '44 – May '45, ~15 nodes, two interleaved shapes).**
- **THE ROADS (if you're out):** the true runner texture — the Sagan station check in the small
  hours, Inky's papers against a torch (KIT decides it); the long walk south in snow; the
  hard-boiled arithmetic of trains versus feet. History fixes the odds: **seventy-three of
  seventy-six are taken.** The player's thread can be any of the record's roads: the cooler and
  back (most), the handoff to the other uniform (the counterfactual license, §6), or — with THE
  CREW and KIT at their best, on the rarest branch — **one of the three** (the Baltic docks and
  a Swedish ship, as two of the real three went; the game's needle-eye ending).
- **THE CAMP (everyone else, and every road returns here in the telling):** the appell that
  won't add up; **von Reyter relieved, arrested, court-martialed** — the roof gone; rumor, then
  **the list under the eagle, forty-one names, growing all week to fifty** (§5 voice sample —
  Vane's name; Inky's name); the urns coming back by rail; **the memorial the Germans allowed
  the prisoners to build** (real, and it stands today). And then Doc's answer to the silence:
  **GEORGE** — the real fourth tunnel, under the theater, dug by men who have just learned
  exactly what digging costs, with no destination and no plan. Our narrator names it: *"We lost
  George over Essen. Then we lost fifty more. This one isn't going anywhere. It's just ours."*
  The bar with no end mark. NERVE becomes the whole game as 1944 grinds — and then **the 3 a.m.
  order, January '45: the camp marches west through snow ahead of the Front** (the real Long
  March). The men the garden kept steady now keep each other alive on the road, counted like
  SOUTH counts MEN. Liberation; the boat; a country that says "at least you sat it out safe" —
  and what a man does with that. *Emotional key: what the digging was for.*

**FRAME CLOSE:** every road returns to the tablecloths in 1994, and the granddaughter, and the
hands. Then the afterword: **"The Fifty."**

## 9. Endings (~15, all Elegy register) & deeds

**The roads out:** **The Needle's Eye** (the docks, a Swedish hold, one of the three — rarest,

to the old man telling it); **The Kept Place** (you go, he stays — and the telling keeps
flinching from the window where he watched you cross the wire).

**The camp roads:** **The Garden** (never out — but George dug, the compound brought through
the March with the named cast counted, SOUTH-graded); **The Road West** (the March as the final
ordeal); **The Long Appell** (NERVE collapse — the wire ending, written with terrible
gentleness, under the counterfactual license); **The Quiet Hook** (the informer/interrogation

**The true ending — "ANSWERING THE ROLL."** Cross-run capstone, gated on THE LOG complete
(every named man's story witnessed across retellings). At the 1994 reunion, the club secretary
reads the roll as it is read every year — and this time, for every name, the last man can
answer. He stands, at 74, at attention, and answers **"Here"** for each of them, because he is
the only one left who can, and because — the Log complete — he finally knows he told all of it
true. The real Fifty's names follow on the afterword page, unchanged, so the player leaves the
fiction holding the record. Final line, granddaughter's hand in his: *"All present. All
accounted for."*

## 10. Art & audio direction

- **Art: "gouache on airmail paper."** Muted period palette — RAF blue-grey, pine green, grey
  topsoil, thin-paper cream; **amber only for memory and home** (letters, the frame, the brew);
  and **yellow — the sand — as the danger color**, invading frames exactly as HEAT rises (the
  real camp's tell, made into the game's visual alarm). No regalia closeups, per §2. ~16 scene
  stills + ending cards + cast portraits via the house Higgsfield sheet-slice pipeline (nano
  2cr/image — full pass ≈ 50–70cr; budget-check `transactions` first, per standing rule). Art
  is a LATER milestone; SVG fallbacks first, as ever.
- **Audio: house WebAudio synth leitmotifs** (no period songs — all melodies original).
  **George's theme**: the crew's whistled tune, four bars, learned in Act I over Essen, earned
  everywhere after. **The tunnel**: low earth drone, breath, boards creaking overhead, the
  trolley's rope-creak. **Home**: a music box that detunes as letters thin. **Appell**: snare
  and cold air. **And the list gets the shelf's only silent leitmotif** — from the first rumor
  to the last name, all music stops. The loudest thing the audio engine will ever do is
  nothing.
